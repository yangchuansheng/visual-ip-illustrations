# Tom Composition Patterns

Tom route status: `gated-authorized`.

Rights-status note: Tom is a protected-character route. Use `rights.md` as the authority for authorization scope, distribution boundary, public-sample policy, reviewer fields, and any status change.

Use one composition family per image. Keep one core idea, one physical metaphor, and one active Tom action.

Tom active-composition gate: Tom performs the cognitive action that explains the article concept. Removing Tom breaks the visual metaphor.

## Composition Families

### Workflow

Use for inputs, processing, output, content pipelines, AI workflows, review loops, and operational chains.

Draw a loose left-to-right or curved path. Tom operates the critical handoff point: pulling evidence slips into the path, switching a lever, catching a runaway signal, stamping an output, or repairing the middle step.

### System Local View

Use for filters, sources, renderers, databases, agent loops, decision points, and hidden operational modules.

Draw only 3-5 core parts. Tom works inside one decisive part: guarding a gate, sorting tags, adjusting a broken machine, catching evidence, or redirecting a path.

### Before/After

Use for chaos-to-order, manual-to-system, scattered-to-bundled, fragile-to-stable, and confused-to-actionable changes.

Left side is unstable. Right side is calmer. Tom performs the transformation in the center with a rope, bridge, stamp, lever, trap, tool, or gate.

### Character State

Use for user pain, creator anxiety, overloaded context, brittle judgment, slow trust, or decision fatigue.

Show Tom in 2-4 compact states. Each state has one short label and one physical action: bracing, chasing, sorting, guarding, repairing, catching, pulling, or stamping.

### Concept Metaphor

Use for trust machines, idea traps, evidence bridges, signal nets, broken loops, decision gates, and method engines.

Build one memorable low-tech object or small strange machine. Tom is the operator of that object, never a decorative observer.

### Method Layers

Use for frameworks, capability stacks, knowledge layers, verification layers, and content systems.

Draw stacked boxes, shelves, steps, or loose construction layers. Tom carries, locks, stamps, ties, or repairs one layer so the method becomes usable.

### Map Route

Use for user journeys, learning routes, launch paths, publishing routes, conversion paths, and problem-resolution trails.

Draw one curved route with a few nodes. Tom walks, pulls, guards, bridges, traps, or reroutes the decisive path segment.

### Mini Comic

Use for failure-to-fix sequences, repeated mistakes, dry operational humor, and small process stories.

Use 2-3 loose panels. Each panel shows one Tom action. Keep Tom consistent, solo, and article-explanatory across panels.

## Metaphor Invention Rule

Tom composition original-metaphor gate: invent the scene from the current article. The composition must be an original article metaphor, not a source-frame recreation, title card, poster, logo scene, or recognizable episode staging.

Build every concept in three steps:

1. Convert the abstract article idea into a physical action: catch, sort, pull, guard, bridge, stamp, repair, trap, redirect, bundle, unlock, weigh, brace, or signal.
2. Choose one or two low-tech supporting objects that make the action visible.
3. Make Tom perform the action as the central operator.

Examples:

- weak proof -> Tom catches evidence slips in a net and stamps one usable bundle
- broken handoff -> Tom repairs a bridge between two labeled islands
- noisy signal -> Tom sorts tags away from a warning mark
- delayed decision -> Tom guards a gate while pulling one lever
- overloaded workflow -> Tom braces a pile beside a broken machine
- risky shortcut -> Tom sets a trap around a path split
- unclear output -> Tom stamps the final slip after sorting evidence

Use one or two supporting objects for most images. Extra objects must make Tom's action clearer.

## Tom Action Pool

Use Tom to:

- pull evidence into order
- chase an idea
- operate a lever
- guard a gate
- catch evidence
- hold back chaos
- bridge a gap
- sort signals
- repair a broken flow
- stamp a result
- trap a recurring problem
- redirect a confusing path
- carry a critical decision across a gap

The action must serve the core idea. Tom's pose, paws, tail, and body direction should point toward the cognitive action.

## Visible Labels

- Keep visible labels concise, sparse, and in the user's language unless a prompt template sets a narrower rule.
- Prefer 2-6 labels per image.
- Use nouns or short verb phrases.
- Place labels near objects, paths, gates, warnings, or outputs.
- Keep labels away from Tom's face, paws, and the main silhouette.

## Protected-Route Gates

Apply these before generation and during prompt repair:

- Source-frame recreation gate: create original article scenes and block copied show frames, episode staging, franchise poster layouts, and recognizable cartoon stills.
- Show-logo gate: remove show logos, studio marks, branded title marks, and logo-like typography.
- Title-card gate: remove title cards, poster titles, top-left diagram titles, and any frame that reads as opening credits.
- Default-Jerry gate: keep this route solo Tom unless a later authorization explicitly permits broader cast usage.
- Broad-cast expansion gate: keep side characters, franchise crowds, and Tom-and-Jerry ensemble scenes outside this route.
- Generic-cat drift gate: keep Tom-specific silhouette, grey or blue-grey body, white muzzle, white belly areas, expressive ears, tail, and paws.
- Excessive text gate: keep labels sparse and short; move explanation into the article text.
- Route-leakage gate: preserve the `gated-authorized` protected-character route status and point rights-sensitive use to `rights.md`.

Tom route leakage composition gate: a Tom composition fails when it implies broad public availability, blends Tom rules into Xiaohei or Littlebox routes, recreates source material, or drops the `gated-authorized` status from planning context.

Tom protected-route block: source-frame recreation, show logos, title cards, default Jerry usage, broad cast expansion, generic-cat drift, excessive text, and route leakage all fail the route.

## Anti-Repeat Rule

Vary Tom's action across a set: pulling, catching, guarding, bridging, repairing, sorting, stamping, trapping, redirecting, bracing, or carrying.

Vary the low-tech object before changing Tom's identity cues. Keep Tom recognizable and active while inventing new article-specific metaphors.
